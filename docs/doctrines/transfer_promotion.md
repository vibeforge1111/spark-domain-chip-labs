# Doctrine: Transfer Only After Two Independent Wins

The lab exists to discover methodology that transfers across chips, not to canonize anecdotes. A change should only be promoted from local tactic to lab doctrine after it produces at least two independent wins in different contexts, because a single improvement often reflects local scaffolding quirks, evaluator bias, or one domain's unusually friendly data shape. Therefore transfer promotion should happen in three steps: prove inside one chip, reproduce in a second chip with a different domain profile, then extract the reusable mechanism and boundary into a shared packet.

This doctrine matters because the lab is upstream of the portfolio. Once a methodology claim is promoted, it risks becoming a default assumption for scaffolders, evaluators, or advisory outputs. If that claim was actually narrow, the lab exports bias at ecosystem scale. The promotion bar should therefore be evidence of mechanism, not mere correlation. A transfer packet should state what changed, why it worked, what failed before, what other chips it was tested against, and when the mechanism should not be reused.

The boundary is that not every insight needs two wins before it is useful. Exploratory ideas can still live in frontier lanes, and urgent regression fixes can be adopted locally before broad validation. The rule is about promotion into durable shared doctrine, not about suppressing experimentation. When the evidence count is below two independent wins, the artifact should remain provisional and labeled as such.

The payoff is a real moat. If the lab becomes the place where reusable chip-building knowledge is promoted only after multi-chip proof, then its packets, scaffolds, and recommendations compound in reliability instead of merely growing in volume.

## Promotion Mechanism

Two-win promotion improves doctrine quality because it separates mechanism from coincidence. When a tactic works once, the result may reflect evaluator luck, temporary context, or a domain-specific shortcut. When the same tactic improves a second chip with a different profile, the lab gains evidence that the mechanism survives domain shift. That second proof therefore converts a local success into a candidate shared rule.

This process also prevents memory pollution. If every local win were promoted immediately, the packet library would grow faster but become noisier, and downstream recommendations would degrade because weak lessons would sit beside strong ones without clear distinction. Requiring a second independent win slows promotion but improves trust, because the lab can explain not only what worked, but why it worked twice and where it stopped working.

Because the lab sits upstream of future chips, this doctrine also improves ecosystem compounding. A carefully promoted rule enables better scaffolds, sharper suggestions, and more reliable transfer packets. A prematurely promoted rule causes the opposite: it spreads local noise across the portfolio and makes later contradiction cleanup more expensive. Therefore the cost of patience is smaller than the cost of polluted doctrine.

Because a second win reduces uncertainty, promoted doctrine starts future chips closer to a reliable baseline. That advantage only holds when the second win is genuinely independent; otherwise apparent transfer is just local pattern repetition and the portfolio inherits a fragile rule.
